Liberal Arts Colleges and the “OPEN KNOWLEDGE INITIATIVE”

October 19, 2001

 

The Open Knowledge Initiative, or OKI, is a collaborative effort among several institutions to create an open source learning management system.  The effort is being led by MIT and Stanford with contributions by several research universities (Michigan, Washington, UC-Berkeley, Princeton, Harvard, Dartmouth, Wisconsin, Penn and others).  The OKI has received significant support from the Andrew W. Mellon Foundation.

 

Susan Perry (Mellon), Michael Roy (Wesleyan), Scott Siddall (Denison) and John Stuckey (Washington & Lee) participated in a two-day workshop (Oct. 15-16 at MIT) to define the tools and features of the OKI,  the initial version of which is due to be released later next year.  This is a very ambitious project, both in scope and timetable.  It's receiving the attention of highly specialized and talented instructional technologists and programmers, but our discussions made clear its awesome complexity.  The designers are striving to make it better and more flexible than any products now available, while also ensuring that it can be used when desired to complement rather than supplant those products.

 

We were there to ensure that the OKI has features relevant to the liberal arts mission and residential style of learning.   The results of a recent poll of CLAC schools were incorporated into our contributions.  We defined dozens of features in four major areas of the OKI:  content management, assessment tools, communication and back-office integration issues.  We paid special attention to the content issues raised by librarians--the need to add electronic content easily in areas such as electronic reserves and the need for electronic reference infrastructure and tools. 

 

Software developers are already writing code for basic services.  The basic tools and the overall user interface will be prototyped in the first quarter of 2002.  The design should be sufficiently flexible to meet a broad range of needs for content creation, management, delivery and testing.  A series of “signature tools” (uniquely powerful but possibly difficult to develop) will be contributed by the larger universities as the project develops. 

 

The OKI is likely to create and influence the next generation of learning management systems in many ways.  The forward-looking features and approach of the OKI have been informed by broad experiences with commercial products such as Blackboard, WebCT and Prometheus.  Most residential, liberal arts colleges should find value in the OKI once it is in production.  While there is no requirement that we will contribute resources to the development effort, it is possible that some of our locally-developed tools are relevant to this effort.  Let one of us know if you feel this is the case.

 

In addition, those of our group of liberal arts colleges who are interested could serve as testers for the products. For example, Stanford is developing a portfolio tool as part of the OKI. Many of our institutions are now moving toward electronic portfolios for students. Some like Kalamazoo, have required electronic portfolios for their students for several years. They, and others who are now moving toward electronic portfolios might be excellent test beds for some of the individual OKI tools as well as for the overall concept.

 

 

Those of us attending the workshop will continue to focus on ensuring that key issues are addressed, such as faculty and system support (especially for an open source product), integration of local resources, ease of use concerns and more.  This is a great opportunity for us to learn in great detail about what some of the larger schools are doing, and in particular, to learn about particular tools (either home grown or vendor supplied) that they use. We welcome your suggestions and questions about the project.

 

Please feel free to contact any of us for more information, or visit the OKI Project web site at http://web.mit.edu/oki for more general information.
