Why Analyze Environmental Samples?
 What is the purpose of environmental sampling and analysis?
 To obtain a small but informative portion of the population being investigated
 You can’t sample the whole environment.
 There are an  infinite number of potential samples.
 Therefore, representative (or unrepresentative)       samples are taken and conclusions about the site are made based on these selected samples.
Samples That Don’t Meet Your Objectives Are Not Worth Analyzing
 If they can’t provide required information, then why collect and analyze them?
 Planning for informative sampling is an                                        integral part of any environmental study.
Data Quality Objectives (DOOs) define what
Measurement Quality Objectives (MQOs) define how
What Causes DQOs to Fail?
 Incomplete planning results in failed or
unrealistic DQO goals.
 Sampling sites are more inaccessible than expected, don’t provide expected type or quality of samples, or cost more to sample.
 Analytical methods have matrix effects (poor analyte recoveries, unexpected interferences).
 Costs of sampling & analysis are too high.
 Data doesn’t meet DQO requirements.
Seven Stages of DQO Planning
State  the  Problem  to  be  Resolved
Identify  the  Decision  to  be  Made
Identify  All  the  Inputs  to  the  Decision
Narrow  the  Boundaries  of  the  Study
Develop  a  Decision  Rule
Develop  Uncertainty  Constraints
Optimize  the  Design  for  Obtaining  Data
Step 1 - State the Problem
to be Resolved
Examples:
 To determine if specific pollutants are present at a site above stated regulatory concentration limits.
To track changes in concentration of specific pollutants at a site over time.
 To determine if a factory needs additional wastewater process changes to meet regulations.
Step 2 - Identify  the  Decision 
to  be  Made / Actions to be Taken
 What exactly will be done if specific pollutants are present at a site above the stated action levels (i.e.,  concentration limits for a chemical)?
 Do additional cleanup at the landfill?
 Prohibit fishing / swimming in the lake?
 Notify residents of the drinking water supply?
 Take more frequent samples at the monitoring wells?
 Fine the factory emitting pollutants from the stack?
Step 3 - Identify All the Inputs
to the Decision
 This is often a difficult step; details needed.
 What data is needed to make decisions?
 Physical characteristics (pH, color, turbidity, temp.)? weather measurements? Specific chemical identifications? Chemical concentrations also? What detection levels? What sample matrices (ground water, industrial wastewater, soil, sediment, air, food)?
 How will the needed data be obtained?
 What methods will be used? Are the detection levels needed achievable? Is the method verified for the matrices?
Step 4 - Narrow the Boundaries
of the Study
 Narrow spatial boundaries
Where will the samples be collected?
 All or portions of a land site? Upstream 1 Km and downstream every 0.5 Km for 10 Km? Along the shoreline 5 Km from an industrial outfall? Every 1 Km for 25 Km downwind from a stack plum? Samples of food from one area of a city, the whole city, multiple cities?  What logistical issues could prevent sampling?
Step 4 (continued) Narrow the Boundaries of the Study
 Narrow the temporal boundaries
When and how long will samples be collected?
 Before and after a land site has been cleaned up? Before and after a new treatment at a factory has been installed? Once a year for 5 years?, Once a month for a year?, Once a day for a week?, Every time it rains during this season? Every time the smog level reaches a certain concentration this year? Every time the field has been sprayed with pesticides this summer?
Step 5 - Develop Decision Rules
 Decision Rules are “IF THEN” actions.
IF “A” happens THEN take Action B, Otherwise take Action C.
IF “A” does not happen, THEN take action B
…and more
Step 6 - Develop Uncertainty Constraints
 Uncertainty constraints are limits on the amount of error in the data that you will tolerate.
 They are used to establish performance goals for design of the data collection activity.
 These limits should be based on the consequences of making a wrong decision.
 e.g., human health risk
Step 6 - (Continued)  Develop Uncertainty Constraints
 Environmental data always have some degree of error associated with them
 Public and decision makers often do not realize this fact.
 Environmental data only provides estimates of the “true” situation. For example:
 You never analyze all the water in a lake or pond, all the air, all the food, etc..

Someone Must Determine What MQOs are Acceptable
 Someone must decide what MQOs are acceptable (US EPA, a State Regulator, you).
How many false positives or negatives are OK?
How precise does the data need to be?
 How accurate does the data need to be?
How else will you know if you succeeded in your study?
Higher Confidence Levels Require More Samples and Higher Costs
In general, more stringent measures of data quality result in higher costs.
The degree of certainty for data quality should take into account the consequences if decisions are wrong.
Confidence Levels Should Be Logical Rather Than Arbitrary
 The question of relative degree of confidence is often arbitrary.
 Everyone likes to start out with a high degree of confidence (for example, 99% or 95%)
 The higher the confidence the more the cost.
 More samples are needed to obtain higher statistical confidence levels.
 Often you may not be able to afford the confidence in the data that you would like because of  budgets.
Step 7 - Optimize the Design for Obtaining Data
 A satisfactory design for obtaining data to meet your specific objectives is rarely achieved the first time.
 Iteration is usually needed.
 If not with Steps 1 - 5, usually at least Step 6.
 Almost always iteration is needed with the numbers of samples to be analyzed because that affects the statistical confidence levels of the data and confidence may have to be decreased to meet budget limitations.
